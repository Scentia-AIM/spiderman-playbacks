What we found

This page summarises the clearest research findings, what they mean for us, and what we are changing as a result.

No action is required at this stage.More detail will be shared as the July sessions are confirmed, especially where input is needed for brand direction, structure, wireframes, copy and experience functionality.

Nightjar's research was concentrated on consumer journeys because self-service is a core part of the B2C/B2C2B experience. They interviewed existing students across the brands, as well as people who chose to study elsewhere. Track 2 (AI-guided) and Track 3 (telesales cue cards) were not the main focus of Nightjar’s remit, but related assumptions were validated through the research.

At a high-level, we confirmed the following assumptions are true:

Students are shortlisting before they speak to us

Many prospects eliminate providers before enquiry, and this persona is growing in size. Our channels need to help them compare, trust and decide earlier.

Self-serve is more important than expected

Discovery points to heavier investment in self-serve discovery and structured content, and less immediate emphasis on AI-guided selling.

Our people create confidence; our other channels don't

Students consistently rated our staff highly. Move routine guidance online so staff can focus on advice, reassurance and complex conversations.

Key research insights

The implications differ across Consumer and Enterprise. Consumer insights are driving the clearest programme direction, with a focus on improving self-service, making product information easier to find and understand, and ensuring it is structured so customers can easily access and compare our offerings through both traditional search and AI-powered tools.

B2C

Consumer

What the findings mean for students and customers.

Our customer journey fails earlier

The friction is more upstream than expected. Customers are often self-eliminating before they reach enquiry, application or purchase, reducing the size of our funnel.

  • AIM students need faster browsing and comparison, but the bigger issue is gated course detail.
  • ABS is typically not even making the shortlist due to perceived credibility, lack of Fee-Help clarity and poor commercial positioning.
  • IECL pathway clarity is too opaque online; sales calls or events are making the pathway concrete.
  • ACHW confidence is being created by sales relationships more than by the website.
What we are changing

Invest more heavily in self-serve web presentation. Customers expect help when comparing and differentiating courses through layout, visual hierarchy, and structured information instead of dense written content.

Many customers already use AI, but wouldn't use ours.

A salient research signal is that many students and customers download information from us, then use tools like ChatGPT or Perplexity to generate their shortlist independently.

For these customers, the experience is effectively zero-touch. They may never want to speak to sales, and they may not need our own AI-guided channel if the public web already gives them enough structured, trustworthy information to evaluate us.

What we are changing

Prioritise making the catalogue AI-readable and rich with structured data, over expecting customers to engage directly with our custom agents outright. This includes clearer on-page course detail, accurate pricing breakdowns for qualifications and comparison signals.

Gating information is hurting customer confidence.

The new sites need to do more confidence-building digitally before a person steps in.

Several students preferred PDFs to the website because the PDF contained the practical detail they needed. In some cases, basic course information was hidden behind forms, downloads or long faculty guides.

  • Open up course detail and stop treating useful course information only as a lead-capture trigger.
  • Make downloadable course information a first-class on-page asset.
  • Surface delivery mode, prerequisites, price, GST treatment, discounts, credential and outcome clearly.
No change since initially scoped
The portal affects student confidence more than expected.

“Purchase complete” is not the end of the journey. The portal is where confidence can wobble: unclear status and delays in seeing course or graduation information create avoidable support load.

  • Show the course immediately after enrolment, even if the start date is weeks away.
  • Make assessments a top-level navigation item.
  • Add clearer status tracking for assessment, marking, certificates, accreditation and graduation.

To prevent drop off, onboarding and early portal experience are being treated as part of the sales journey rather than as a separate downstream environment.

No change since initially scoped
Human support should be used where it adds the most value.

Students consistently valued our people, to the extent several outright mentioned their intent to drop out until helped by a progression manager.

The issue reported by students is that staff are too often explaining pricing, clarifying pathways, chasing status, or manually guiding people through steps that should be easier online.We need to move routine guidance into the digital journey so human support can focus on complex advice, exceptions and moments where reassurance genuinely matters.

No change since initially scoped
B2B

Enterprise

What the findings mean for account-led selling.

Relationships are doing too much work

A strong finding for enterprise is our relationships are needed where they create value without being present during 'busywork'. To accomplish this, we need to shift routine interactions into self-service so our people can focus on the conversations only they can have, including:

  1. understanding emerging capability and workforce needs
  2. advising on workforce development strategies
  3. recommending the right learning pathways
  4. designing solutions that achieve client outcomes.

Rather than spending valuable relationship time explaining pricing structures, outlining course options, managing enrolments, issuing reports, answering learner status questions, and coordinating administrative processes, these routine interactions should increasingly be handled digitally.

No change since initially scoped
Content transparency shapes longlists

Content transparency matters before sales engagement because this is where many Enterprise customers generate their longlists.

Several Consumer findings translate directly into Enterprise: gated course information, no variable pricing guides, inconsistent course detail, and limited comparison functionality all make it harder for buyers to build internal confidence before they directly engage us.

No change since initially scoped
Client reports must demonstrate value
Mid-term win

Budget holders must be able to easily defend their spend, see ROI, and understand value delivered through us. Their need mirrors the need for clear progression on the consumer side, and require us to overhaul our portal envrionment to demonstrate their next best actions.

  • Make reporting easier to access through self-service.
  • Reduce manual reporting requests and status chasing.
  • Support stronger renewal and expansion conversations.
Pricing guidance can be tested earlier
Short-term win

Enterprise customers appreciate providing visibility on a watered-down and conservative pricing model, and it helps us shape their expectations on what to expect prior to human engagement.

For example: if I have 10 staff on two-day courses, cost is X. If I have 100 staff, discounts are Y. The same content should specify service inclusions so buyers can form a credible internal view before contacting us.

What we are changing

The research changes emphasis. Project Spiderman remains a connected web, AI, telesales and onboarding programme, but the next phase will lean harder into self-serve foundations before expanding customer-facing AI guidance.

The biggest shift: We are increasing investment in self-serve discovery, structured content, comparison, site architecture and AI-readable catalogue data. We are reducing the near-term emphasis on AI-guided selling as the primary digital guidance channel.
More self-serve

Ungated course detail, clearer pathways, comparison tools, transparent pricing, stronger calls to action and better post-purchase guidance.

Better AI visibility

Content structured so Google, ChatGPT browsing, Perplexity and other tools can understand and represent our courses accurately.

More useful human handoff

Sales and support step in with context when value is highest, rather than restarting conversations or explaining basic information.

What happens next

There are three main July activities coming out of discovery.

1

User flow definitions

We will define how customers move through the future experience from discovery and comparison through to enquiry, purchase, onboarding and ongoing support. This work establishes the information architecture, page relationships and navigation patterns that underpin the entire experience before detailed design begins.

2

Enterprise and Consumer UX guides

The tactical brand discovery has now been completed and will be distilled into a concise UX brand guide for AIM. This one-page document translates the brand's vision, customer expectations and commercial priorities into practical design principles that will guide the visual identity, interaction patterns and overall user experience of the new sites.

3

Low-fidelity wireframes (preview screens)

Low-fidelity wireframes will begin. Copy is the major dependency: Nightjar can design the page structures and layouts, but Scentia needs to provide the exact copy the designs must accommodate. This will cover course pages, landing pages, forms, calls to action, support content, onboarding content and related journey messaging. I will communicate separately about what is needed, who will contribute, and how we will coordinate it.
